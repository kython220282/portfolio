AI Glossary for Everyone

Top AI terms that every AI and ML professional must know
November 2025
‎ Table of Contents
  • Key Terms

Key Terms#

For each term, we’ll provide:

  • I (Interview Definition): A concise, professional explanation suitable for technical interviews.
  • L (Layman Definition): A simple analogy or explanation that a 10-year-old can understand.

Artificial Intelligence(AI)

  • I: AI is the simulation of human intelligence by machines to perform tasks like learning, reasoning, and decision-making. For example, self-driving cars use AI to navigate and respond to traffic conditions.
  • L: Imagine teaching a robot to think like you and make smart choices—like a friend who learns from you and helps you with homework.

Machine Learning(ML)

  • I: A subset of AI where computers learn from data without explicit programming, improving performance over time. An example is email spam filters learning to identify unwanted emails through examples.
  • L: This is when computers learn from lots of examples instead of being told exactly what to do.

Deep Learning(DL)

  • I: A complex form of ML using neural networks with multiple layers to model intricate patterns in data. For instance, deep learning powers speech recognition in virtual assistants like Siri or Alexa.
  • L: A special kind of machine learning where computers learn from data by using many layers, like a brain. It helps computers recognize your voice on phones.

Neural Network

  • I: A computing system inspired by the human brain’s network of neurons that processes data in layers for pattern recognition. Used in image classification, such as identifying objects in photos.
  • L: A computer system that works a bit like our brain and helps computers understand things like pictures or sounds. Like a program recognizing a cat in a photo.

Large Language Model (LLM)

  • I: AI models trained on vast text data to understand and generate human-like language. ChatGPT is an example of an LLM used for conversational AI.
  • L: A big computer program trained on lots of text so it can talk and write like a person. ChatGPT is an example that can chat with you.

Generative AI

  • I: AI that creates new content such as images, text, or music based on learned patterns. DALL·E generates images from textual descriptions as a generative AI example.
  • L: AI that can make new things like pictures, stories, or music from what it has learned. Like an app that turns words into drawings.

Transformer

  • I: A neural network architecture designed to process sequential data efficiently, mainly used in language models. It enables models like BERT to understand context in sentences.
  • L: A smart computer method that helps understand sentences better than older ways. It helps voice assistants understand complex questions.

Reinforcement Learning (RL)

  • I: AI learns to make decisions by receiving rewards or penalties for actions, improving through trial and error. An example is an AI mastering video games by playing repeatedly.
  • L: Computers learn by trying things and getting rewards if they do well, like a game where you get points for good moves.

Natural Language Processing (NLP)

  • I: Technology that enables machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language. Chatbots use NLP to converse naturally with users.
  • L: When a computer understands words and sentences like a human to have a conversation. Siri and Alexa use this to talk to you.

Supervised Learning

  • I: ML technique where models are trained on labeled data containing input-output pairs. Image recognition trained on photos tagged with object names exemplifies this.
  • L: The computer learns by looking at many examples where the answer is known, like learning to recognize dogs by seeing labeled dog pictures.

Unsupervised Learning

  • I: ML where models find patterns in unlabeled data without explicit instructions. Clustering customers by buying behavior without prior labels is an example.
  • L: The computer tries to find patterns in data without answers, like grouping animals by their features without being told their names.

Transfer Learning

  • I: Reusing a pre-trained model on a new related task to save time and resources. Fine-tuning a model trained on general images to identify medical X-rays.
  • L: A computer uses what it learned from one job to help with a new, similar job. Like learning to play one video game, then getting better at another.

Fine-Tuning

  • I: Adjusting a pre-trained model on specific data to improve accuracy for a particular task. Modifying a language model to better understand legal documents.
  • L: Changing a computer program a little to make it better at one special task. For example, tuning a speech program to understand accents.

Tokenization

  • I: Breaking text into smaller units like words or subwords for easier processing by AI models. Splitting the sentence “I am AI” into tokens “I”, “am”, and “AI”.
  • L: Breaking up sentences into small pieces, like words or parts of words, so computers can understand better. Like splitting “I love AI” into “I,” “love,” “AI.”

Embedding

  • I: Mapping words or items to numerical vectors that capture semantic meaning, aiding AI understanding. Word embeddings let AI know “king” and “queen” are related concepts.
  • L: Turning words into numbers that show what they mean, so computers can understand relationships. It helps computers know “cat” and “kitten” are similar.

Overfitting

  • I: When a model learns training data too exactly, failing to generalize to new data. A model that memorizes exam answers may fail on unseen questions.
  • L: When a computer learns too much from the examples and can’t handle new ones well. Like memorizing a test and doing poorly on a new one.

Underfitting

  • I: When a model is too simple and fails to capture underlying data patterns. Using a linear model to predict complex nonlinear trends can cause underfitting.
  • L: When a computer is too simple and can’t learn well enough, missing important details. Like guessing without really studying.

Hyperparameter

  • I: External settings that control how an AI model learns, like learning rate or number of layers. Choosing the right hyperparameters improves model performance.
  • L: Settings that control how a computer learns, like adjusting volume or brightness on your device.

Explainable AI (XAI)

  • I: AI designed to make its decisions understandable to humans for trust and transparency. A credit scoring AI explaining why a loan was approved or denied.
  • L: AI that can explain why it made a decision so people can understand it, like a robot telling you why it picked a certain game.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

  • **I:**Hypothetical AI with human-level cognitive abilities across many tasks, not just one. AGI would reason and learn as broadly as a person, though it does not yet exist.
  • L: A computer smart enough to do any task people can do, like a really clever robot friend (still only in imagination).

Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)

  • I: Two neural networks competing, one generating fake data and the other detecting it, improving output realism. GANs create realistic synthetic images used in art and media.
  • L: Two AI models play a game: one makes fake pictures, and the other tries to spot fakes, making the pictures better over time.

Chatbot

  • I: AI systems designed to simulate conversation with humans through text or speech. Customer support bots help answer queries on websites using chatbot technology.
  • L: A computer program you can talk to that answers questions or helps you, like a virtual assistant on a website.

Prompt Engineering

  • I: Crafting specific inputs to guide AI into producing desired and accurate outputs. Using tailored prompts to get precise answers from a language model.
  • L: Giving clear and smart instructions to an AI so it gives the best answers, like asking a robot the right question.

Synthetic Data

  • I: Artificially generated data used for training AI when real data is limited or sensitive. Simulated driving data helps train autonomous vehicle systems.
  • L: Fake data made by computers to help teach AI when real data is hard to get, like drawing practice pictures for a robot.

Few-Shot Learning

  • I: AI’s ability to learn new tasks with very few examples, reducing data needs. A model recognizing new objects after seeing only a handful of images.
  • L: When an AI can learn a new thing after seeing only a few examples, like learning a new game quickly.

Multi-Agent System

  • I: Multiple AI agents that interact and collaborate to solve problems or complete tasks. Robotic teams coordinating warehouse logistics exemplify this.
  • L: Many AI “robots” or programs working together to solve a problem, like a team winning a game by helping each other.

AI Bias

  • I: When AI reflects or amplifies unfair prejudices due to biased data or design, harming fairness. Facial recognition systems misidentifying certain demographic groups highlight AI bias concerns.
  • L: When AI makes unfair decisions because the examples it learned from were unfair, like a robot that doesn’t treat everyone equally.

Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

  • I: Automating repetitive business tasks using software robots to increase efficiency. Automating invoice processing in finance departments using RPA tools.
  • L: Using computer robots to do boring and repeated office work, like typing data over and over.

Conversational AI

  • I: AI systems designed for natural dialogue with humans, such as virtual assistants. Alexa and Google Assistant are examples of conversational AI that understand and respond via voice.
  • L: AI that talks and listens to people naturally, like Alexa or Google Assistant helping you with questions.

Autonomous AI

  • I: AI systems capable of operating independently without human intervention. Self-driving cars navigate and make driving decisions autonomously.
  • L: AI that can work by itself, like a self-driving car that knows where to go without a driver.
